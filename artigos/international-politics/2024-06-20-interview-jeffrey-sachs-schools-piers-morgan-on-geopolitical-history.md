## **Jeffrey Sachs Schools Piers Morgan on Geopolitical History: A Lesson in US Hypocrisy and Broken Treaties**

Piers Morgan is joined by Professor Jeffrey Sachs to discuss Vladimir Putin's fifth term as Russia's President, the ongoing war in Ukraine and who he thinks is to blame, America's potential TikTok ban, along with Israel's actions in Gaza.

Sachs criticises the United States who he says is responsible for exacerbating tensions with Russia, particularly through support for Ukraine and NATO expansion.

He argued that the overthrow of the Yanukovych government in 2014 was a critical turning point that led to the current crisis in Ukraine. By pushing for regime change and destabilizing the region, the US inadvertently contributed to the escalation of the conflict and strained relations with Russia.

In a heated debate, economist and geopolitical expert Jeffrey Sachs delivered a masterclass in geopolitical history to [Piers Morgan](https://youtu.be/c586OPde_NA?si=dVz2-S49-JjniKnO), dismantling the narrative that Russia alone bears responsibility for the Ukraine conflict. Sachs, drawing on decades of firsthand knowledge of US foreign policy, exposed a pattern of American interventions, illegal wars, and broken agreements that have destabilized regions from the Balkans to the Middle East—and set the stage for the current war in Ukraine. His critique challenges the West’s moral posturing, calling for transparent negotiations to end the conflict and prevent further escalation.

## The US Legacy of Illegal Interventions

Sachs begins by rejecting Morgan’s accusation of being overly sympathetic to Putin’s worldview, instead pointing to the US’s own history of “barbarism” in global conflicts. He cites the 1999 US-led NATO bombing of Belgrade—conducted without UN authorization—as the first major European conflict after World War II. The 78-day campaign aimed to break Serbia and create Kosovo as a NATO enclave, establishing Bondsteel, the largest NATO base in the Balkans. This set a precedent for the US to flout international law, a pattern repeated in Afghanistan, Iraq, Syria, and Libya.

In Syria, Sachs highlights the Obama administration’s role, with Hillary Clinton tasking the CIA to overthrow Bashar al-Assad, leading to a prolonged civil war. Similarly, the 2011 NATO bombing of Libya, which toppled Muammar Gaddafi, lacked UN approval and plunged the country into chaos. Sachs’s critique underscores a US foreign policy that prioritizes regime change over stability, often with catastrophic consequences.

## The 2014 Ukraine Coup: A US-Orchestrated Overthrow

Sachs provides a firsthand account of the 2014 Maidan coup in Ukraine, where the US played a pivotal role in overthrowing President Viktor Yanukovych. He recounts how, on February 21, 2014, the EU brokered an agreement with Yanukovych for early elections, a government of national unity, and a stand-down of both sides—an accord also supported by Russia and the US. Yet, the very next day, right-wing Ukrainian forces, backed by the US, stormed government buildings and deposed Yanukovych. Within hours, the US endorsed the new regime, ignoring the EU agreement and the unconstitutional nature of the coup. This betrayal, Sachs argues, ignited the conflict in eastern Ukraine, as ethnic Russians in Donbas rejected the new government in Kiev.

## The Minsk II Betrayal: A Treaty Doomed by Western Deception

The 2015 Minsk II agreement, unanimously adopted by the UN Security Council, was intended to end the conflict by granting autonomy to Donbas and fostering peace through negotiations. Signed by Ukraine and guaranteed by Germany and France, the treaty was a legally binding path to de-escalation. However, Sachs reveals that the US undermined it from the start, privately assuring Ukraine it didn’t need to honor the autonomy provisions. Angela Merkel’s 2022 interview with *Die Zeit* confirmed this duplicity, admitting that Minsk II was merely a “holding pattern” to buy time for Ukraine to build its military strength—an admission that shocked Sachs, given the treaty’s international backing. This betrayal, he argues, exemplifies the West’s untrustworthiness and contributed to the 2022 escalation.

## A Call for Transparent Negotiations

Sachs’s core argument is that neither side—Russia nor the US—can be trusted without transparent, public agreements. He calls for both parties to negotiate openly before the world, laying out clear terms: no more regime changes, defined boundaries, and a halt to NATO expansion. Such transparency, he believes, would allow the global community to hold both sides accountable, potentially restoring faith in international treaties. Sachs’s distrust of the US government, based on its history of broken promises, fuels his insistence on this approach. He warns that without such measures, the cycle of conflict and mistrust will persist, risking further global instability.

## Reckoning with the West’s Role in Global Conflict

Jeffrey Sachs’s debate with Piers Morgan serves as a stark reminder of the US’s role in destabilizing regions through illegal interventions and broken agreements. From the Balkans to Ukraine, the US has prioritized geopolitical dominance over international law, often with devastating consequences. Sachs’s call for transparent negotiations challenges both Russia and the West to break this cycle, offering a path toward peace that prioritizes accountability over propaganda. As the Ukraine war continues, his lesson in geopolitical history urges a reevaluation of the West’s moral narrative, demanding a commitment to genuine diplomacy to prevent further escalation.
