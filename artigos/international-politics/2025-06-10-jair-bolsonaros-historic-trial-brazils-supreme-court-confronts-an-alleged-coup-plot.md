## Jair Bolsonaro’s Coup Trial: Brazil’s Supreme Court Case Explained

In a defining moment for Brazilian democracy, former President Jair Bolsonaro faces an unprecedented trial before the Supreme Court (Supremo Tribunal Federal, STF) on charges of orchestrating a coup to overturn his 2022 election defeat to Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. The case, rooted in allegations of a far-right conspiracy to undermine democratic institutions, marks the first time since Brazil’s military dictatorship (1964-1985) that a former president and high-ranking military officers face such grave accusations in a civilian court. Drawing on official Brazilian judiciary sources, including the STF’s portal, this article explores the trial’s background, charges, proceedings, and implications.

## Background: A Polarized Nation and a Contested Election

Jair Bolsonaro, who served as Brazil’s president from 2019 to 2022, lost the October 2022 presidential election to Lula by a narrow margin. His refusal to concede, coupled with claims of electoral fraud—despite no evidence—fueled tensions. On January 8, 2023, thousands of Bolsonaro supporters stormed Brazil’s Congress, Supreme Court, and presidential palace in Brasília, demanding Lula’s ouster. Federal investigations, led by the Federal Police and overseen by STF Justice Alexandre de Moraes, uncovered evidence of a broader plot allegedly involving Bolsonaro and his inner circle.

On March 26, 2025, a five-justice STF panel unanimously ruled that Bolsonaro and seven associates must stand trial for their roles in the alleged coup attempt. The decision, detailed on the STF’s official portal, followed a federal police investigation that identified Bolsonaro as the leader of a “criminal organization” active since at least 2021.

## The Charges: A Threat to Democracy

Bolsonaro faces five counts, as outlined in the STF’s rulings:

- Attempting to Stage a Coup: Accused of planning to overthrow Lula’s government through a “state of siege” or military intervention.

- Involvement in an Armed Criminal Organization: Allegedly leading a network that included former ministers and generals.

- Attempted Violent Abolition of the Democratic Rule of Law: Plotting to dismantle democratic institutions.

- Damage Characterized by Violence and Serious Threat Against State Assets: Linked to the January 8, 2023, attacks on government buildings.

- Deterioration of Listed Heritage: For damage to Brazil’s cultural and institutional landmarks during the riots.

If convicted, Bolsonaro could face up to 40 years in prison, a sentence unprecedented for a former Brazilian president. The STF’s portal notes that 33 others, including former ministers and military officers, are also charged, highlighting the case’s scope.

## The Trial: Proceedings and Key Developments

The trial began on May 20, 2025, with the STF hearing key witnesses via videoconference. A former army commander testified that he discussed a “state of siege” with Bolsonaro after the election, a revelation that strengthened the prosecution’s case. By June 1, 2025, the STF had finalized the deposition phase, with over 80 witnesses providing testimony. On June 6, 2025, Justice Alexandre de Moraes rejected a defense request to suspend the trial, confirming Bolsonaro’s interrogation for June 9, 2025, as announced on the STF’s official X account.

The STF’s portal emphasizes the trial’s historic nature, noting that it is the first time since 1985 that high-ranking military figures face civilian justice for coup-related charges. The court’s unanimous decisions reflect a commitment to accountability, despite Bolsonaro’s claims of “political persecution” and “judicial activism.”

## Health Complications and Public Reaction

Bolsonaro’s health has complicated the trial. Since April 2025, he has been hospitalized multiple times for intestinal issues stemming from a 2018 campaign-trail stabbing. On April 24, 2025, while in intensive care at DF Star Hospital in Brasília, he received a court summons to present his initial defense within five days. The STF justified the timing, citing Bolsonaro’s public activities, including a YouTube broadcast from his hospital bed, as evidence of his capacity to respond.

Public sentiment is deeply divided. Supporters rallied in São Paulo on April 6, 2025, demanding amnesty for Bolsonaro and those arrested for the January 8 riots. Meanwhile, progressive groups view the trial as a reckoning for Bolsonaro’s tenure, marked by environmental destruction, attacks on Indigenous rights, and a mishandled COVID-19 response that led to hundreds of thousands of deaths. Posts on X reflect this polarization, with some claiming the trial is a “witch hunt,” while others see it as a step toward justice.

## Implications: Democracy on Trial

The trial’s outcome will shape Brazil’s democratic trajectory. A conviction could deter future attempts to undermine elections, reinforcing the judiciary’s role as a guardian of the rule of law. However, critics warn that a lengthy sentence risks further polarizing the country, potentially boosting Bolsonaro’s popularity among his base. The Financial Times noted that the case could “divide the country” if mishandled, while the STF’s rigorous process aims to ensure transparency.

The STF’s handling of the case has drawn international attention, with some right-wing figures, including Bolsonaro, comparing it to “judicial activism” in other countries, such as France’s case against Marine Le Pen. Yet, the STF’s portal underscores the evidence-based nature of the proceedings, rooted in federal police findings and witness testimonies.

## Looking Ahead

As Bolsonaro’s interrogation unfolds, the trial is expected to conclude by late 2025, with a verdict potentially before year-end. The STF’s commitment to a swift yet thorough process signals Brazil’s resolve to address threats to its democracy. For a nation with a history of unpunished coups, this case is a test of whether accountability can prevail.
