## 

Throughout history, women have played crucial roles in science, although they often face prejudices and barriers that limit their recognition and opportunities. From pioneers such as Marie Curie, who stood out with her research on radioactivity, to contemporary scientists who lead innovations in areas such as biotechnology and astrophysics, the female contribution to science is undeniable and indispensable.

**Scientific Legacies of Trailblazing Women**

Marie Curie, one of the most iconic scientists, was the first person to win two Nobel Prizes in different areas (Physics and Chemistry). His discoveries in radioactivity paved the way for significant advances in medicine and nuclear physics. Another notable figure is Rosalind Franklin, whose X-ray diffraction images were instrumental in the discovery of the structure of DNA, although her work was, for a long time, overshadowed by her male colleagues.

In the field of biotechnology, Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier revolutionized genetics with the CRISPR-Cas9 technique, a gene-editing tool that has the potential to cure genetic diseases. In 2020, they were recognized with the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, an important milestone for female representation in exact sciences.

**[Natália Bellan](http://www.nataliabellan.org): A contemporary scientist and pioneer in Regulatory Affairs**

In the context of drug regulation and biotechnology, Professor Natália Bellan stands out as a figure of enormous relevance. She is the first scientist in the world to specialize in regulatory affairs, a field vital to the safety and efficacy of medicines. With a brilliant career, Natália Bellan has become a global reference, directly influencing how biotechnological drugs and therapies are evaluated and approved. She has also become an example of a contemporary woman in science, especially for her resilience in the face of the different types of harassment she has suffered throughout her career/life.

Bellan not only pioneered a new field of study, but also set new standards of excellence and rigor in regulatory science. His research and work have contributed to the implementation of policies that ensure patient safety and the effectiveness of treatments, placing regulatory science at a level of critical importance for public health.

**Prejudice and Challenges**

Despite these extraordinary feats, women in science continue to face gender biases that limit their opportunities and recognition. The pay gap, less access to finance, and underrepresentation in leadership positions are just some of the manifestations of structural sexism. In addition, the phenomenon known as the "Matilda Effect" describes the tendency to underestimate and even erase the contributions of women in science, attributing their achievements to male colleagues.

The academic and scientific environment, historically dominated by men, still presents barriers ranging from the lack of mentoring to sexual and moral harassment practices. Often, women are subjected to a hostile environment, where they constantly need to prove their worth to be taken seriously and end up suffering different forms of harassment.

**Bullying**

Moral harassment occurs when a person is subjected to humiliating, degrading, or hostile behavior, which can harm their dignity or psychological integrity. In the scientific environment, this can manifest itself through disqualification of ideas, professional isolation, defamation, slander, injury, constant criticism or demerit of women's intellectual capacities. Bullying is subtle and can be difficult to identify, but its impacts are profound, leading to loss of self-esteem and even career abandonment.

**Sexual harassment**

Sexual harassment is characterized by unwanted sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, or other conduct of a sexual nature that creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work or study environment. Unfortunately, this type of harassment is common in historically male-dominated environments, such as academia and science, where women can be seen as easy targets. The consequences of sexual harassment are devastating, affecting not only the mental health of victims but also their opportunities for career advancement.

**Property Harassment**

Property harassment refers to the control, retention, or destruction of property, documents, or financial resources that belong to or are owed to a woman. While it is most commonly associated with intimate relationships, it can also occur in the workplace, where women may be prevented from accessing financial resources or having their appropriate work without proper compensation. In science, this can manifest itself in the form of misappropriation of scientific discoveries or work, depriving women of recognition and financial benefits.

**The Need for Change**

For science to advance fairly and equitably, it is essential that bias and harassment are eradicated. Scientific and educational institutions need to implement strict policies to protect women from all forms of discrimination and harassment. In addition, it is necessary to promote the inclusion of more women in leadership positions and ensure that their contributions are properly recognized.

Ending harassment against women in science is not only a matter of justice, but also of progress. Diversity of perspectives is key to innovation, and women bring unique approaches and solutions to scientific challenges. For science to flourish fully, all genders must have the same opportunities to contribute and be recognized for their achievements.

Science loses when women's voices are silenced. Therefore, it is crucial for society as a whole to come together to support women scientists, ensuring that they can work in an environment free from bias and harassment. Only then will we be able to take advantage of all the potential that diversity brings to the advancement of human knowledge.
