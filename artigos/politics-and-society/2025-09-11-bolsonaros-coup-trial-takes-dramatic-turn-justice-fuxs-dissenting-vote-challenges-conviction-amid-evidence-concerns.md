## Bolsonaro Coup Trial 2025: Justice Fux's Acquittal Vote Slams Lack of Evidence in Dramatic Supreme Court Split

In the heart of Brasília, where the marble halls of Brazil's [Supreme Federal Court](https://noticias.stf.jus.br/postsnoticias/ap-2668-terceiro-a-votar-ministro-luiz-fux-abre-divergencia-em-relacao-ao-voto-do-relator/) (STF) echo with the weight of history, a pivotal chapter in the nation's turbulent political saga unfolded on September 10, 2025. Former President Jair Bolsonaro, the firebrand leader whose tenure from 2019 to 2022 polarized the country like few before him, stood at the center of a high-stakes trial accusing him of masterminding a coup d'état following his electoral defeat. As the five-justice panel neared its verdict, the proceedings took an unexpected turn: Justice Luiz Fux, the court's only career judge with a background rooted in the judiciary rather than political appointments, delivered a blistering dissenting vote. Casting doubt on the very foundation of the case, Fux argued for Bolsonaro's acquittal, decrying what he saw as a trial built on shaky evidence and procedural missteps. This moment not only fractured the bench but also reignited debates about justice, power, and the rule of law in Latin America's largest democracy.

To grasp the gravity of this development, we must rewind to the events that set the stage. Bolsonaro's presidency ended amid controversy in December 2022, when he lost a razor-thin runoff election to Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. Refusing to concede gracefully, Bolsonaro's supporters stormed government buildings in Brasília on January 8, 2023, in scenes reminiscent of the U.S. Capitol riot two years earlier. The STF, Brazil's highest court, launched investigations into whether Bolsonaro and his allies—including high-ranking military officers—had orchestrated a plot to overthrow the new government. By 2025, the trial had evolved into a sprawling affair, charging Bolsonaro with forming a criminal organization, attempting a coup, and related crimes. Prosecutors from the Attorney General's office painted a picture of conspiracy, citing Bolsonaro's inflammatory rhetoric, meetings with military leaders, and alleged plans to invoke emergency decrees to nullify the election results.

The trial's final deliberations began in early September 2025, with Justice Alexandre de Moraes—known for his tough stance against Bolsonaro's circle—serving as the reporting judge. Moraes voted to convict, followed by Justice Cármen Lúcia, who concurred, setting the stage for what seemed like an inevitable guilty verdict. But on September 10, Fux shattered that momentum. In a marathon session lasting over 13 hours, Fux meticulously dismantled the prosecution's case, arguing that without an actual overthrow of the government, there could be no coup d'état. He dismissed much of the evidence as circumstantial, pointing out that Bolsonaro had not had full access to investigative materials—only those cherry-picked by the prosecution. "This is a judgment without proofs," Fux reportedly emphasized, his words carrying the authority of a jurist who rose through the ranks from a state judge to the STF, unlike his colleagues appointed from academia or politics.

Fux's background as the "único juiz de carreira" (sole career judge) on the court amplified the impact of his dissent. Appointed in 2011 by President Dilma Rousseff, Fux has often positioned himself as a defender of procedural integrity, earning respect across ideological lines for his emphasis on due process. In this vote, he contended that the STF lacked jurisdiction, insisting the case belonged in lower courts since Bolsonaro was no longer in office—a technicality that could annul the proceedings entirely. His upset expressions during the session, directed at interruptions from peers, underscored the tension on the bench. This split—now 2-1 against Bolsonaro, with two votes pending from Justices Gilmar Mendes and Dias Toffoli—has injected uncertainty into a trial many saw as a foregone conclusion.

The narrative of this trial is one of high drama, blending legal intricacies with the raw emotions of a nation still healing from political wounds. Imagine the scene: Bolsonaro, barred from running for office until 2030 due to a prior TSE ruling on electoral misconduct, watching from afar as his fate hangs in the balance. His defense team, buoyed by Fux's vote, hailed it as a "reinforcement of democracy," arguing it exposes flaws in what they call a politically motivated witch hunt. Bolsonaro himself, in a defiant post on X (formerly Twitter), declared, "The truth will prevail," echoing his supporters' chants of judicial bias. Protests erupted outside the STF, with Bolsonaro loyalists waving flags and decrying "judicial dictatorship," while counter-demonstrators demanded accountability for the January 8 riots that injured dozens and damaged national symbols.

This dissent arrives at a critical juncture for Brazil. Under President Lula's second term, the country grapples with economic recovery post-COVID, inflation hovering at 4.5%, and social divisions exacerbated by Bolsonaro's enduring popularity among evangelicals and the military. If convicted, Bolsonaro faces up to 12 years in prison, further sidelining him from politics and potentially galvanizing his base for future unrest. Fux's vote, however, opens a door for appeals, possibly delaying resolution and allowing Bolsonaro to maintain his influence through media appearances and rallies. Legal experts like constitutional scholar Conrado Hübner Mendes have praised Fux for upholding evidentiary standards, warning that a conviction without solid proof could erode public trust in the judiciary.

Internationally, the trial draws parallels to U.S. cases against Donald Trump, Bolsonaro's ideological ally. Trump himself commented on Truth Social, calling it a "sham trial" and urging support for "our Brazilian friend." Human rights groups like Amnesty International have monitored proceedings, emphasizing the need for impartiality amid Brazil's history of military coups. The case also ties into broader investigations: Bolsonaro's passport was seized in February 2024 over coup allegations, and he's ineligible for office until 2030 following a TSE decision in June 2023 for spreading election misinformation.

As the remaining justices prepare their votes—expected by mid-September—the nation holds its breath. Mendes and Toffoli, both seen as swing votes, could tip the scales. Fux's intervention has already shifted the narrative, from inevitable guilt to a contested battle over justice's essence. For Bolsonaro's detractors, it's a frustrating delay; for supporters, a vindication. Yet, beneath the legal jargon lies a human story: a former leader fighting for his legacy, a court divided, and a country questioning if healing is possible without truth.

This trial isn't just about one man; it's a litmus test for Brazil's democracy. Fux's courageous stand against what he views as evidentiary voids reminds us that justice must be blind, not bent. As proceedings resume, the world watches, hoping for resolution in a saga that has defined a decade of division.
